1. Quick verdict

The short version. This is a really good family home for two young kids — modern, four bedrooms, pool, theatre room. But it sits on a small 369 m² block (about half the typical Padbury size), and once you account for the pool, there's almost no grass for the kids to play on. The asking price is fair if we're buying for the building. It's not great if we wanted long-term land value growth.

Buy it if: the kids are young, we want a low-maintenance modern home, we love the pool, and we're not planning to renovate or extend.

Walk away if: we want a real backyard for kids to run around, we want subdivision/extension potential, or the final price goes above $1.30M.

3. Why this house works for our family

  • Four bedrooms + a study — each kid gets their own room, and we have a home office or guest room.
  • All bedrooms upstairs together — kids close to us while they're young.
  • Theatre room — kid movies, homework, playroom that contains the mess.
  • Parents' retreat upstairs — a quiet space for us in the evenings when the kids are in bed.
  • Pool — huge in Perth summers.
  • It's in a cul-de-sac (Brahe Place is a court) — low traffic, safe for kids.
  • Rear of the house faces roughly north — the gold standard in Perth (winter sun, summer shade).
  • Walking distance to Padbury Primary and the local park.
  • Modern, near-new build — much less maintenance than a 1970s Padbury home.

4. What we should be careful about

  • Tiny backyard. 369 m² minus the house and the pool leaves almost no grass. If we want the kids running around outside, this house is not ideal.
  • It's almost certainly a "survey-strata" block — meaning the original block was split into two and we'd be sharing a title scheme with the neighbour. Less freedom to extend or change things.
  • Smaller land = less long-term price growth. Padbury's strong growth comes mostly from the big blocks that can be subdivided. We can't do that here.
  • Pool means ongoing cost. Maintenance, chemicals, power, and a fence compliance inspection every 4 years.
  • Two storeys = stairs. We'd need baby gates if our youngest is still toddling.
  • The dining area is small (3.7m × 2.4m) — fine for the four of us, tight when family visits.
  • Hot market = pressure. Padbury houses sell in about 7 days. We may need to offer fast and firm.

6. Questions to ask Brett (the agent)

Tap each one to see why we're asking and what different answers mean for us. Print this page or open all questions before the open home so we don't miss anything.

1What kind of title is this — green title, survey-strata, or strata?+
Legal Cost Deal-breaker
Why we're asking The "title type" decides how much freedom we have over the property. With a 369 m² lot, it's almost certainly not green title — it's been sub-divided. We need to know which kind of sub-division.
What each answer means for us
  • "Green title" → ideal but unlikely on this size lot. We'd own the land outright. No shared anything. Most freedom, best resale.
  • "Survey-strata" → the most common for this type of block in Padbury. We own our piece of land separately, just sharing a survey plan with the neighbour. Usually no ongoing fees and minimal restrictions. This is fine.
  • "Strata title" → we'd share insurance and pay a quarterly fee to a strata company. Less freedom to renovate. Less attractive on resale. Ask how much the fees are.
2When was the house built and when was the pool installed?+
Building Cost
Why we're asking Age tells us roughly how much repair we're likely to face in the next 10 years. It also affects insurance, warranty, and electrical/plumbing standards.
What each answer means for us
  • Under 10 years → very few maintenance surprises. May still have some structural warranty. Great.
  • 10–20 years → likely modern wiring/plumbing, but appliances (oven, dishwasher, aircon) may be near end of life. Budget $5–15k for replacements in the first 5 years.
  • Over 20 years → we'd want a detailed building inspection. Possible repainting, roof, hot water, aircon replacements within a few years.
  • Pool over 15 years old → liner/tiles may need replacement (cost: $8–25k). Pump and filter likely on borrowed time.
3What primary school is this address zoned for?+
Location Deal-breaker
Why we're asking Padbury has four primary schools. The catchment line decides which one our kids go to for 7 years. Some addresses on Brahe Place go to Padbury Primary, others to Bambara Primary.
What each answer means for us
  • Padbury Primary → ~600 m walk, established community school.
  • Bambara Primary → ~1.2 km, also good reputation, slightly newer.
  • "You'd have to check" → we verify ourselves at schoolsonline.det.wa.edu.au before making the offer. Never trust the agent's word on this — it's the buyer's responsibility.
4What are the council rates and water rates per year?+
Cost
Why we're asking These are ongoing costs that come bundled with the property — we pay them every year for as long as we own it. They're never optional.
What each answer means for us
  • Council rates $1,800–$2,400/year → normal for Padbury. Fine.
  • Council rates above $2,500/year → unusual for a 369 m² block. Worth questioning.
  • Water rates $1,200–$1,500/year → typical. With a pool, expect the higher end.
  • "I'll have to check" → fair answer. We'd see exact figures in the Section 32-equivalent contract before signing.
5Are there any strata fees? If yes, how much per quarter?+
Cost Legal
Why we're asking If this is strata (not survey-strata), there are ongoing fees we'd pay every quarter forever. Most survey-strata don't have fees, but some have a small contribution for shared insurance.
What each answer means for us
  • "No fees" → great. Likely survey-strata with no shared property.
  • Under $300/year → fine — usually just shared insurance.
  • $300–$1,500/year → noticeable annual cost. Ask what it covers.
  • Over $1,500/year → unusual for a duplex pair. Ask if there's a special levy planned (a one-off big charge for repairs).
6Does the pool have a current compliance certificate?+
Legal Cost
Why we're asking In WA, pool fencing has to be inspected by the council every 4 years. If it fails, the owner has to fix it before selling — but sometimes it gets pushed to the buyer.
What each answer means for us
  • "Inspected within the last 4 years, fully compliant" → perfect. No action needed.
  • "Compliant but inspection due soon" → fine, just budget $80–150 for the next inspection.
  • "Not compliant" or "We don't know" → could be a $500–$5,000 fix (replacing fencing, gate, latches). We'd want this resolved before settlement, in writing.
7Has the property had any major works or repairs we should know about?+
Building Legal
Why we're asking Past major repairs reveal weak points (water damage, structural issues, roof). Sellers have to disclose known issues — but it's much easier to dodge a question than refuse to answer one.
What each answer means for us
  • "Nothing major" → good, but we still get an independent building inspection.
  • "Minor things — repainted, replaced a hot water system" → totally normal.
  • "Re-stumping / roof restoration / re-plumbing" → could indicate a serious past issue. Ask for documentation and warranty.
  • "Water damage / leak repair" → red flag. We need a thorough inspection and confirmation that the cause was fixed, not just the symptom.
8What type of air conditioning does it have?+
Building Cost
Why we're asking At this price point, we should expect ducted reverse-cycle. Lesser systems mean either retrofitting (expensive) or higher electricity bills.
What each answer means for us
  • Ducted reverse-cycle → ideal. Whole-house heating + cooling. Check the age.
  • Split systems in main rooms → fine but bedrooms upstairs may be hot/cold. Acceptable.
  • Evaporative cooling only → cools but doesn't heat well, and struggles on humid days. We'd want to retrofit (~$8–15k).
  • None / window units → unusual at this price. Walk away or factor a major upgrade.
9Are there solar panels? What size, and is there a battery?+
Cost
Why we're asking Solar in Perth saves serious money — easily $1,200–$2,500 a year off our power bill. A house with solar already installed is worth more than one without.
What each answer means for us
  • 6.6 kW+ system with battery → big plus. Could cut our electricity bill almost to zero.
  • 6.6 kW+ system, no battery → still excellent. Saves $1,200–$2,000/year.
  • Smaller system (3–5 kW) → some savings. Could expand later.
  • No solar → not a deal-breaker, but we'd plan to install it within 6 months (~$5–10k upfront, pays back in 4–6 years).
10What's included in the sale — appliances, blinds, pool equipment?+
Legal Cost
Why we're asking Anything not listed as included can legally be removed by the seller before settlement. We've heard stories of light fittings, blinds, even garden plants being taken.
What each answer means for us
  • Fixed items, blinds/curtains, dishwasher, oven, pool cleaner all included → standard expectation. Get it written into the offer.
  • "Dishwasher / fridge not included" → ok, but factor $1–3k each into our moving budget.
  • "TVs, garden statues, light fittings excluded" → fine if specified upfront, awful if discovered after settlement.
Action: Whatever Brett says, we list every "included" item explicitly in our written offer — every appliance, every blind, the pool cleaner, the air conditioning units.
11Why is the owner selling, and how long have they owned it?+
Negotiation
Why we're asking The seller's situation tells us how flexible the price might be, and whether there might be a hidden problem.
What each answer means for us
  • "Upgrading" / "Downsizing" / "Moving for work" → normal. Seller is rational. Negotiation depends on competition.
  • "They've already bought another house" → strong negotiation leverage for us. They need to sell.
  • "Owned for less than 2 years" → unusual. Ask politely why. Could be life event (divorce, work) or could be an undisclosed issue with the house or street.
  • "Estate sale" / "Deceased estate" → executors usually want a clean and quick sale. Often negotiable.
12How many offers have you received? When's the deadline?+
Negotiation
Why we're asking This shapes our offer strategy. In a hot market, agents use offer counts as pressure. We need to know if it's real pressure.
What each answer means for us
  • "No offers yet" → we're in a strong position. Can offer slightly below asking with conditions.
  • "One or two offers" → competition exists but not heated. Offer at the lower end of our range.
  • "Multiple offers" → we need to decide fast. Offer at our true ceiling or walk.
  • "There's a deadline this Friday" → may be genuine, may be a tactic. Stay disciplined to our $1.30M ceiling regardless.
Remember: The agent works for the seller, not for us. Their job is to get the highest price. Be polite, but don't let urgency push us past our limit.
13Are there any easements, encumbrances, or council notices on the property?+
Legal Deal-breaker
Why we're asking An easement is a legal right someone else has over part of our land — like a drain or a power line — that we can't build over. A council notice could mean unapproved building work that we'd inherit.
What each answer means for us
  • "Nothing unusual" → standard. We'd still verify with our conveyancer when reviewing the contract.
  • "There's a drainage easement at the rear" → very common in survey-strata. Means we can't build over a small strip. Usually fine.
  • "Unapproved patio / pergola" → we ask seller to obtain retrospective council approval before settlement, in writing. Could cost them $1–5k and delay settlement.
  • "Pending council notice" → red flag. We need full details before we offer.
14What type of NBN is connected here?+
Building
Why we're asking Working from home, kids' schoolwork, streaming — internet quality matters. NBN type varies a lot by Perth suburb.
What each answer means for us
  • FTTP (fibre to the premises) → best. Up to 1000 Mbps. Future-proof.
  • FTTC (fibre to the curb) → very good, up to 250 Mbps.
  • FTTN (fibre to the node) → speed depends on distance to the local node. Could be slow.
  • HFC (cable) → fast but congestion-prone in the evenings.
  • "I don't know" → we check ourselves at nbnco.com.au using the address (takes 30 seconds).
15Anything new being built or planned near here that buyers should know about?+
Location
Why we're asking Quiet streets can become noisy if a multi-storey development goes in opposite, or a new road is widened. The agent often knows about local developments because other neighbours have asked.
What each answer means for us
  • "Nothing I'm aware of" → fine. We can verify by checking the City of Joondalup development applications register.
  • "A few subdivisions happening" → normal for Padbury, doesn't usually affect quality of life much.
  • "Apartment development / commercial build / road widening" → big impact on traffic, noise, and resale value. Investigate carefully.

How the two strategies actually differ

Strategy 1 — Keep renting + investing
  • Keep our $800k portfolio fully invested
  • Pay rent each month (out of income — no other housing costs: no council rates, no insurance, no pool maintenance, no major repairs)
  • Keep contributing $4,000/month into Vanguard
  • Everything compounds in shares
Strategy 2 — Buy 1 Brahe Place
  • Use ~$308k of our portfolio for deposit + stamp duty + costs. Portfolio drops to ~$492k.
  • Pay mortgage every month for 30 years + ongoing house costs (~$1,000/month for rates, insurance, pool, etc., growing with inflation)
  • House appreciates over time; mortgage gets paid down → equity builds
  • Likely can't keep investing $4k/month — the mortgage eats most of our investing capacity. Default below is $0/month; you can change it if you think you can still invest some.
The honest trade-off: We give up $308k upfront + the ability to compound $4k/month for 30 years. In return we get a house that appreciates (tax-free as primary residence) and forced equity through mortgage repayments. How the compounding math works (tap to expand)+

Rent strategy — each month:

  1. Existing portfolio grows by one month's share return (e.g. 7%/12 = 0.583%)
  2. Add $4,000 new investment
  3. Result becomes the starting balance for next month

Formula: portfolio = portfolio × (1 + 0.07/12) + 4000, repeated 360 times for 30 years. Both the original $800k AND every new $4k contribution compound for as long as they're in the portfolio.

Example for month 1: $800,000 × 1.00583 + $4,000 = $808,667. Month 2: $808,667 × 1.00583 + $4,000 = $817,381. And so on.

Buy strategy — at start:

  • Pay deposit + stamp duty + costs upfront from the $800k portfolio
  • Take out the mortgage for the rest of the purchase

Buy strategy — each month:

  1. Pay mortgage (split between interest and principal — principal builds equity, interest doesn't)
  2. Pay ongoing costs (rates, insurance, pool, etc.) — growing with inflation
  3. House value grows by one month's appreciation rate
  4. Remaining portfolio grows by one month's share return, plus whatever new investing we add

Total wealth at any point = (current house value − remaining mortgage balance) + remaining portfolio.

What the math doesn't capture

These numbers tell us a lot, but they leave out things that matter just as much:

  • Stability for the kids. No risk of being asked to move because the landlord is selling, no rent reviews, no inspections. Big deal when they're in school.
  • Forced savings. Mortgage payments build equity automatically. Most people invest less than they plan to — buying removes that risk.
  • Tax advantages of the family home. No capital gains tax when we eventually sell. Our $800k in Vanguard will incur CGT (with the 50% discount for >12-month holdings).
  • Concentration risk. Buying turns our diversified portfolio into one big bet on a single 369 m² block in Padbury. Shares stay diversified across thousands of companies.
  • Liquidity. Selling shares takes 3 days. Selling a house takes 3 months and costs ~3% in agent + duty + legal fees.
  • Flexibility. If we want to move overseas, change jobs, or relocate for the kids' education, the share portfolio comes with us. The house doesn't.
  • Maintenance time. A house takes weekends. Shares don't.
  • The emotional value of "our own home" — painting walls, planting trees, knowing it's ours. That's real even if it doesn't show up in a spreadsheet.
The honest takeaway: The math is closer than most people think. With realistic assumptions, the financial difference between renting+investing and buying is usually within ±10–20% of total wealth at year 30 — meaningful, but not as decisive as either side claims. The decision is really about lifestyle, stability, and what we want our daily life to look like — not just dollars.

11. How we should decide

Here's a simple way for us to know which side of the fence we're on:

Decision A — buy 1 Brahe Place: if we agree that "modern home with pool" matters more to us right now than "big backyard". The kids are young, we want low maintenance, and we love the floor plan. We cap our offer at $1.30M.
Decision B — keep looking: if we agree that a real backyard with grass is non-negotiable for our kids' next 10 years, or if the title turns out to be strata with meaningful fees, or if the asking price climbs above $1.30M.
The one question to ask each other after the open home:
"Can I picture our kids playing here for the next ten years?"

If both of us say yes, this is our house. If either of us hesitates, we keep looking.
